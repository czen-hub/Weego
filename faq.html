Real answers about how WeGo works — the fees, the pension, the insurance, and the plan. No fluff.

Section 1 — Membership & Fees

There are two costs: a one-time founding membership fee of $500, payable over 12 months ($41.67/month), and a $75/month membership due that covers your commercial insurance, app infrastructure, and seeds your retirement trust.

The $500 is your cooperative share — it buys you ownership, voting rights, and your founding member status. It is not a deposit that gets refunded; it is your entry into the company. The $75/month replaces what Uber silently keeps from your fares every single day.

The math: A typical Uber driver doing $5,000/month in gross fares pays Uber ~$2,750 in platform fees (55%). A WeGo member doing the same volume pays $600 in fees (12% tech + $75 membership). That's $2,150 more in your pocket every month.

The monthly $75 dues are separate and remain steady — adjusted annually by the Consumer Price Index (CPI) to protect the retirement trust from inflation. If you leave and rejoin, you restart at $500, not at the level you left. This creates a powerful incentive to stay.

Your $75/month is allocated across four functions:

The retirement trust portion is legally protected in a separate trust account — it cannot be used for operating expenses, marketing, or any other business purpose. It is yours.

For context: if you do just 9 rides per month at an average $30 fare, the math already favors WeGo over Uber. Every ride beyond that is a gain on top.

If you need to pause driving (illness, family, off-season), you may pause your membership for up to 3 months per year while preserving your vesting progress and cooperative shares. The $75/month is not charged during an approved pause.

Section 2 — The 20-Year Driver's Pension

The Driver's Legacy Fund is a pension program modeled after the military's 20-year service retirement. A driver who joins in 2026 and completes 20 years of active membership by 2046 becomes eligible for ~$2,500/month for the rest of their life.

The funding source is the cooperative's Autonomous Vehicle fleet. By Year 10, WeGo will own 50,000 AVs through the Retirement Trust. Each AV generates approximately $4,000–$6,000 in net monthly profit. A portion of that income is assigned to each vested retiree — meaning the robots do the work, and the human who built the company collects the check.

The math: A driver who starts at 25, drives for 20 years, and retires at 45 invests roughly $22,500 total (fees over 20 years). They then receive ~$2,500/month — $600,000 over the next 20 years, and continuing for life. That's a 2,600%+ return on their total investment.

The pension is based on 20 years of service membership, not age. If you join at 25 and complete 20 years, you are eligible at 45. The cooperative operates on the Army Model — your commitment is the clock, not your birthday.

At 20 years you receive two things immediately:

  • $20,000 equity payout — your accumulated internal capital account, paid as a lump sum on the day you complete 20 years.
  • ~$2,500/month pension — begins immediately, funded by your assigned AV unit in the fleet.

If you complete fewer than 20 years, partial vesting applies: 3–5 years = 25%, 5–10 years = 50%, 10–15 years = 75%, 15–19 years = 90%. If you stop membership before 3 years, no pension vesting is earned, though your $500 membership share may be redeemable.

20 years — Standard pension~$2,500/mo · non-transferable

For a driver with a family, the 25-year tier generates an estimated $528,000 in income for surviving family members. This is designed specifically for immigrant driver families and multi-income households that depend on the driver's continued earnings.

The Retirement Trust is legally structured as a Qualified Trust under ERISA — the same framework that governs major pension funds in the United States. This means:

  • The trust is a separate legal entity from the cooperative's operating accounts.
  • Management cannot access, borrow from, or invest the trust funds in the business.
  • If the cooperative itself were to face financial difficulty, the trust funds are protected from creditors.
  • The trust is governed by a Joint Board of Trustees — 50% elected driver-members, 50% independent fiduciaries — who have a legal duty to the pension beneficiaries.

Annual actuarial reviews ensure the fund remains solvent at all projected payout levels. Full transparency reports are published to all members every year.

Section 3 — Insurance & Protection

No — and this is one of the most dangerous gaps in the current gig economy. Personal auto insurance almost universally excludes commercial driving. The moment you turn on a rideshare app, your personal policy stops covering you for liability purposes.

Uber provides $1M liability coverage only during Periods 2 and 3 (ride accepted, passenger in car). During Period 1 — when the app is on but you're waiting for a request — their coverage is minimal ($50k/$100k) and in practice requires the driver's personal insurer to be involved first, even though personal insurers deny rideshare claims.

WeGo covers all three periods. Your membership dues fund a group commercial policy that provides primary liability coverage from the moment you turn on the app to the moment you turn it off. No gaps. No "your personal policy first" runaround.

In Year 1, WeGo negotiates a group commercial liability policy with a major carrier (e.g., Progressive, Liberty Mutual) for the initial 1,000 Bay Area drivers. The $500 founding fees provide the deposit required by the insurer — typically 25–30% of the annual premium upfront.

As the cooperative grows to 50,000+ drivers, we transition toward a Risk Retention Group (RRG) — a driver-owned insurance pool that keeps premiums inside the cooperative instead of paying profit to an outside insurer. By Year 7–10, the goal is a fully Captive Insurance Company owned by the cooperative, where any surplus from good driving years returns to the drivers as dividends.

Section 4 — Ownership & Governance

WeGo is structured as a California Worker Cooperative Corporation under the state's cooperative laws. This means:

  • 1 Member, 1 Vote. Every member has equal voting power regardless of how long they've been a member or how many rides they do. A founder with 5 years in doesn't outvote a newer member.
  • Board elections. The Board of Directors is elected by the driver-members. Management serves at the will of the drivers, not outside investors.
  • Profit sharing. At year end, surplus revenue above operating costs is distributed back to members as patronage dividends proportional to their ride activity — not held for shareholders.
  • No outside equity. WeGo does not sell equity to venture capital firms or outside investors. The cooperative is funded entirely by member dues and revenue.

If you leave before 3 years, the $500 founding fee is non-refundable (it was used to build the infrastructure you've been benefiting from). Any partial vesting under 3 years results in no pension entitlement.

If you leave after vesting has begun (3+ years), you retain your partial pension rights at the applicable vesting percentage. Your internal capital account balance (accumulated equity credits) becomes redeemable at the cooperative's next annual book-value calculation.

You cannot sell your membership share to a third party. Cooperative shares are non-transferable to non-members — they must be redeemed by the cooperative at book value. This keeps the cooperative in the hands of working drivers, not speculators.

Section 5 — Autonomous Vehicles & the Future

No — and that's precisely the point. At Uber or Tesla, autonomous vehicles replace human workers and channel the profit to shareholders. At WeGo, the cooperative owns the AV fleet, so when a robot drives a ride, the income goes to the drivers' retirement trust — not to a CEO or a hedge fund.

By Year 6–8, WeGo will use accumulated cooperative capital to purchase AVs in bulk (estimated $55,000 per unit). These vehicles operate 20 hours/day, net ~$4,000/month, and that income is used to fund the ~$2,500/month pensions of the human drivers who built the cooperative.

The model: Every retired human driver is matched to one AV in the fleet. That AV works 24/7 so the retired driver doesn't have to. The robot doesn't replace the human — the robot works for the human.

Human drivers continue to serve the routes and passengers AVs structurally cannot handle: elderly assistance, children, medical transport, complex luggage situations, and high-trust concierge services.

This is a real risk we plan for explicitly. Our strategy against AV market disruption has three layers:

  • The "Complex Mile" defense. AVs excel at simple point-to-point loops. WeGo's human drivers will specialize in assisted rides, school transport, medical logistics, and other high-trust services that automated vehicles cannot provide. These are higher-margin, lower-competition segments.
  • Political moat. WeGo's 500,000+ driver-owners are voters in every city we operate in. We actively support local ordinances requiring that a percentage of all rideshare miles in a city be served by locally-owned cooperative entities. Multiple cities are already exploring this.
  • Infrastructure ownership. If Waymo floods the streets with AVs, those AVs still need to charge and be serviced. WeGo's 200 Service Hubs become the pit crew for the robot revolution. We collect infrastructure revenue from every AV on the road — including competitors' vehicles.
Section 6 — For Riders

Three reasons that matter to most riders:

  • Lower fares. Because WeGo keeps only 12% vs Uber's 55%, we can charge passengers 15–20% less per ride while paying drivers more. No cross-subsidy needed — the math works because we removed the profit layer going to Wall Street.
  • No surge pricing. Ever. WeGo's fares are flat. A ride from SFO to San Francisco costs the same at 8 AM and at 11 PM on New Year's Eve. You will never be punished for needing a ride at a busy moment.
  • Driver quality. WeGo drivers are owner-operators with a stake in the cooperative's reputation. A driver who owns the company has more reason to give an excellent ride than a contractor who will be replaced by an algorithm tomorrow.
