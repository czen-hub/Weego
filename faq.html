Section 2 — The 20-Year Driver's Pension

The Driver's Legacy Fund is a pension program modeled after the military's 20-year service retirement. A driver who joins in 2026 and completes 20 years of active membership by 2046 becomes eligible for a projected ~$2,500/month for the rest of their life.

The Retirement Trust is funded through two tracks that work together:

  • Track 1 — Member contributions + investment returns (the foundation). $25 of every driver's $75/month dues flows directly into the trust. At 500,000 active members, this generates $450M/year in contributions. Invested conservatively at 5% annual return, the trust reaches approximately $5.8B by Year 10 — enough to fund all early retirees entirely from investment returns, with no AV income required.
  • Track 2 — Autonomous Vehicle fleet income (the accelerator). By Year 6–8, WeGo projects owning 50,000 AVs through the Retirement Trust. Each AV targets $4,000–$6,000/month net income. AV revenue is the growth engine that scales pension payouts beyond what contributions alone can fund — and allows pensions to continue even if a retiree outlives contribution-driven projections.

The pension does not depend on AVs arriving on schedule. The trust is solvent from member contributions alone. AVs raise the ceiling. See Section 8 for the full AV risk analysis.

The math: A driver who starts at 25, drives for 20 years, and retires at 45 invests approximately $20,000 total in dues and fees over 20 years. At the projected ~$2,500/month target, they receive $600,000 over the next 20 years — and continuing for life. That's a projected 2,900%+ return on total investment. Projections assume current fee structures, 5% trust returns, and cooperative growth targets — actual results will vary.

The pension is based on 20 years of service membership, not age. If you join at 25 and complete 20 years, you are eligible at 45. The cooperative operates on the Army Model — your commitment is the clock, not your birthday.

At 20 years you receive two things immediately:

  • $20,000 equity payout — your accumulated internal capital account, paid as a lump sum on the day you complete 20 years.
  • Projected ~$2,500/month pension — begins immediately, funded from the Retirement Trust (contributions + investment returns + AV fleet income).

If you complete fewer than 20 years, partial vesting applies: 3–5 years = 25%, 5–10 years = 50%, 10–15 years = 75%, 15–19 years = 90%. If you stop membership before 3 years, no pension vesting is earned, though your $500 membership share may be redeemable.

The pension fund has three layers of protection — each independent of the others:

  • ERISA Qualified Trust (legal isolation). The Retirement Trust is a separate legal entity from the cooperative's operating accounts. Management cannot access, borrow from, or pledge it. If the cooperative faces financial difficulty, the trust is protected from creditors. This is the same legal framework used by major U.S. pension funds.
  • Joint Board of Trustees (governance independence). The trust is governed by a board that is 50% elected driver-members and 50% independent fiduciaries appointed by the trust document. These fiduciaries have a legal duty exclusively to pension beneficiaries — not to cooperative management. Their appointments survive any change in cooperative leadership.
  • Constitutional supermajority lock-in (amendment protection). The ERISA separation of the pension fund is one of five "protected principles" written into the cooperative's founding constitution that require a 75% supermajority vote to amend and cannot be changed at all during the cooperative's first 10 years. A simple board majority cannot vote away pension protections. See Section 7 for the full governance structure.

Annual actuarial reviews ensure the fund remains solvent at all projected payout levels. Full transparency reports are published to all members every year.

Section 3 — Insurance & Protection

No — and this is one of the most dangerous gaps in the current gig economy. Personal auto insurance almost universally excludes commercial driving. The moment you turn on a rideshare app, your personal policy stops covering you for liability purposes.

Uber provides $1M liability coverage only during Periods 2 and 3 (ride accepted, passenger in car). During Period 1 — when the app is on but you're waiting for a request — their coverage is minimal ($50k/$100k) and in practice requires the driver's personal insurer to be involved first, even though personal insurers deny rideshare claims.

WeGo covers all three periods. Your membership dues fund a group commercial policy that provides primary liability coverage from the moment you turn on the app to the moment you turn it off. No gaps. No "your personal policy first" runaround.

In Year 1, WeGo negotiates a group commercial liability policy with a major carrier (e.g., Progressive, Liberty Mutual) for the initial 1,000 Bay Area drivers. The $500 founding fees provide the deposit required by the insurer — typically 25–30% of the annual premium upfront.

As the cooperative grows to 50,000+ drivers, we transition toward a Risk Retention Group (RRG) — a driver-owned insurance pool that keeps premiums inside the cooperative instead of paying profit to an outside insurer. By Year 7–10, the goal is a fully Captive Insurance Company owned by the cooperative, where any surplus from good driving years returns to the drivers as dividends.

Section 4 — Ownership & Governance

WeGo is structured as a California Worker Cooperative Corporation under the state's cooperative laws. This means:

  • 1 Member, 1 Vote. Every member has equal voting power regardless of how long they've been a member or how many rides they do. A founder with 5 years in doesn't outvote a newer member.
  • Board elections. The Board of Directors is elected by the driver-members. Management serves at the will of the drivers, not outside investors.
  • Profit sharing. At year end, surplus revenue above operating costs is distributed back to members as patronage dividends proportional to their ride activity — not held for shareholders.
  • No outside equity. WeGo does not sell equity to venture capital firms or outside investors. The cooperative is funded entirely by member dues and revenue.

If you leave before 3 years, the $500 founding fee is non-refundable (it was used to build the infrastructure you've been benefiting from). Any partial vesting under 3 years results in no pension entitlement.

If you leave after vesting has begun (3+ years), you retain your partial pension rights at the applicable vesting percentage. Your internal capital account balance (accumulated equity credits) becomes redeemable at the cooperative's next annual book-value calculation.

You cannot sell your membership share to a third party. Cooperative shares are non-transferable to non-members — they must be redeemed by the cooperative at book value. This keeps the cooperative in the hands of working drivers, not speculators.

This is a real risk we plan for explicitly. Our strategy against AV market disruption has three layers:

  • The "Complex Mile" defense. AVs excel at simple point-to-point loops. WeGo's human drivers will specialize in assisted rides, school transport, medical logistics, and other high-trust services that automated vehicles cannot provide. These are higher-margin, lower-competition segments.
  • Political moat. WeGo's 500,000+ driver-owners are voters in every city we operate in. We actively support local ordinances requiring that a percentage of all rideshare miles in a city be served by locally-owned cooperative entities. Multiple cities are already exploring this.
  • Infrastructure ownership. If Waymo floods the streets with AVs, those AVs still need to charge and be serviced. WeGo's 200 Service Hubs become the pit crew for the robot revolution. We collect infrastructure revenue from every AV on the road — including competitors' vehicles.

Section 7 — Legal Structure & Member Protections

All founding member fees are held in a dedicated escrow trust account — completely separate from WeGo's operating accounts — until three milestone conditions are met:

  • CPUC TNC license issued
  • Group commercial insurance policy bound
  • Driver app in active beta with 100+ rides completed

Until all three are met, no funds are released to the cooperative for operations. If WeGo fails to reach these milestones within 24 months of your enrollment date, you are entitled to a full refund of any fees paid. This structure is documented in your membership agreement before a single dollar changes hands.

Why this matters: Any platform collecting money from workers in exchange for future benefits — without this structure — creates potential consumer protection liability under California law. We built the escrow in because it protects you, and because it proves to every skeptical driver that we cannot run off with the money.

This is the most important structural question any prospective member can ask. The answer is a three-layer lock:

  • Layer 1 — The ERISA Trust. The Retirement Trust is a separate legal entity from the cooperative. It is not owned by the cooperative, cannot be voted on by the cooperative's board, and is not reachable by the cooperative's creditors. A future board vote cannot touch it because the board has no legal authority over it.
  • Layer 2 — The Cooperative Constitution. Written into WeGo's founding documents are five "protected principles" that require a 75% supermajority vote to amend, and cannot be changed at all during the cooperative's first 10 years: (1) the 12% tech fee ceiling, (2) ERISA separation of the pension fund, (3) the 1 Member 1 Vote rule, (4) the prohibition on outside equity, and (5) the member-defined pension payout structure. A simple majority vote of the board can never change these.
  • Layer 3 — Independent Fiduciaries. The Joint Board of Trustees overseeing the Retirement Trust is 50% elected driver-members and 50% independent fiduciaries appointed by the trust document. The independent trustees have a legal duty to pension beneficiaries — not to cooperative management — and their appointments survive any change in cooperative leadership.

This is how credit unions and the most durable worker cooperatives in the world protect themselves from short-term democratic decisions that would destroy long-term value.

Under California Corporations Code Section 12200+, worker cooperative membership interests are explicitly structured as cooperative shares — not investment contracts. A member's economic benefit comes from their own labor (the rides they drive), not from the efforts of others. This is the legal distinction that separates a cooperative from a securities offering.

What this means practically:

  • Your $500 is a membership share, not an investment expecting passive returns.
  • Cooperative membership is not registered with the SEC and does not need to be.

WeGo is structured with the Sustainable Economies Law Center (SELC) in Oakland — the leading California cooperative law firm — to ensure this distinction is clean and legally bulletproof before any member funds are accepted.

WeGo operates as three separate legal entities, each with a distinct purpose and liability boundary:

WeGo Cooperative Corp (CA)Operating company

Holds the CPUC license, employs staff, runs the app, collects the 12% tech fee and $75 dues. This entity can be regulated, sued, or face financial difficulty. Drivers are member-owners here.

WeGo Retirement Trust (ERISA)Pension fund

Completely separate legal entity. Holds pension assets, governs AV fleet ownership, governed by the Joint Board of Trustees. No management from the cooperative sits on this board. Litigation against the cooperative cannot reach this entity.

WeGo State Chapters (LLCs)State-by-state operations

Each state is its own LLC handling local insurance, regulatory compliance, and driver disputes. A lawsuit in California cannot reach Texas drivers' pension contributions. This is the cellular structure that protects national scale.

This three-entity structure means a catastrophic legal or financial event in one state cannot cascade to destroy the entire cooperative or touch the retirement trust. It is how major pension funds and durable institutions are built.

Death and permanent disability trigger immediate 100% pension vesting, regardless of years served at the time of the event. This is one of the most important protections for immigrant driver families and multi-income households.

  • Death: Full vesting activates immediately. Your designated beneficiary begins receiving the proportional pension payment within 60 days. If you were at 10 years of service at 75% vesting, your family receives 75% of the full pension — immediately, for life.
  • Permanent disability: Same immediate vesting. Monthly disability supplement equal to 50% of your average monthly WeGo earnings over the prior 12 months, for a maximum of 36 months while transitioning, plus the vested pension amount ongoing.
  • Involuntary removal (misconduct): Requires a full board vote with a 60-day appeal period. Pension vesting is frozen at the level earned to date — it cannot be forfeited, only frozen.
